SECTION 03 · VALUE CHAIN

How the chip business works

If you can confidently draw the chip value chain on a whiteboard and explain exactly where IP sits in it, you'll sound like an insider. This is the single most useful page to internalise. It also explains the exact example Vinny walked you through on the call.

In this section

  • Define fabless, foundry, VCA, tape-out, IP, SoC, node
  • Retell Vinny's Netrasemi → IMEC → TSMC example
  • Explain how IP vendors actually make money

The big picture: nobody does it all

Designing and manufacturing a chip is so hard and so expensive that the industry split into specialists. Each plays one role and hands off to the next. Here's the whole chain:

The semiconductor value chain. IP vendors (where you sell) are the very first link.

The key terms, defined from zero

Fabless

A "fabless" company designs chips but owns no factory ("fab"). It focuses on the design and outsources manufacturing. Examples: Qualcomm, Nvidia — and in India, startups like Netrasemi. This is the model India's government is pushing hard (see Section 5). Analogy: a fashion label that designs clothes but hires factories to sew them.

Foundry

A foundry is the factory that actually manufactures the chips on silicon wafers. TSMC (Taiwan) is by far the largest; Samsung, GlobalFoundries, Intel Foundry and UMC are others. Analog Bits works with all of them. The foundry doesn't design chips — it manufactures other people's designs.

IP (Intellectual Property / "IP cores")

A pre-designed, pre-verified building block of a chip that a design company licenses instead of building from scratch. A PLL, a USB interface, a memory block — these are complex to design correctly, so companies buy proven ones. This is what you sell. Analogy: pre-built, tested Lego sub-assemblies a builder drops into their model rather than moulding every brick themselves.

SoC (System on Chip)

A single chip that integrates many functions — processor, memory interfaces, I/O, clocking, sensors — into one piece of silicon. A modern SoC stitches together dozens of IP blocks from multiple vendors. More IP blocks per SoC = more opportunities for you.

Tape-out

The moment a finished chip design is sent to the foundry to be manufactured. Historically the design was written to magnetic tape — hence "tape-out." It's a milestone: after tape-out, changes are extremely expensive, which is exactly why customers want silicon-proven IP they can trust.

VCA — Value Chain Aggregator

A TSMC partner that aggregates many smaller customers so they can access the foundry. A small startup can't get TSMC's attention directly, so it goes through a VCA, which handles tape-out, wafer production, and supply-chain logistics. IMEC is TSMC's VCA in India (based in Bangalore). This is the "IMEC" Vinny mentioned — its India head sends him leads.

EDA (Electronic Design Automation)

The software used to design and verify chips. The giants are Synopsys and Cadence — who also sell IP, which is why Vinny mentioned competing with them. EDA tools are the "CAD software" of chip design.

Process node (e.g. 5nm, 3nm, 2nm)

A measure of how advanced/miniaturised the manufacturing is. Smaller number = newer, denser, lower-power, more expensive. Analog Bits being "silicon-proven at 2nm" means their IP works on the most cutting-edge process — a strong selling point for high-end customers.

Replay of Vinny's exact example — make sure you can retell it "Netrasemi is designing a chip in India. They need IPs, so they come to Analog Bits (us) and license, say, a PLL — they pay a license fee. They integrate it into their design. Now they need to manufacture. But TSMC won't deal with someone that small, so Netrasemi goes through IMEC, the VCA. IMEC handles tape-out → TSMC → finished chips. And because IMEC sees every fabless startup first, its India head sends us leads — so a lot of our business is inbound." If you can retell this cleanly, you've shown you understand the whole chain.

How IP vendors actually make money

License fee (NRE)

An upfront fee the customer pays to license the IP for their design. The bigger, more advanced, more customised the IP, the higher the fee. This is the headline deal size.

Royalty

A per-chip fee paid as the customer ships volume. If their product sells in millions of units, royalties can dwarf the license fee over time. (Not every deal has royalties; some are license-only.)

This is why Vinny stressed 60–70% gross margins versus 20–25% for services. IP is built once and licensed many times — there's no per-unit labour like there is in a services/consulting model. It's why he keeps saying "the deal sizes are pretty large" and "the sky's the limit."

Hard vs. soft IP (a term that will impress)

Worth knowing because M31 emphasises it: IP comes in different forms. Soft IP is delivered as design code (flexible, the customer implements it). Hard IP is a fully laid-out, process-validated physical block ready to drop into silicon — higher reliability, ideal for analog and high-speed blocks. Analog Bits' analog macros and M31's PHYs are essentially hard IP: "silicon-proven, first-time-working." That phrase — first-time-working — is the entire value proposition of paying for proven IP.

Activity: explain the chain in your own words

Try this out loud: A friend who knows nothing about chips asks, "What will you be selling?" Give a 30-second answer that names where IP sits and why customers buy it.

"Chips are built from dozens of pre-made building blocks. Designing each one perfectly is really hard, so chip companies license proven blocks instead of building their own. I sell those building blocks — specifically clocking and power blocks from Analog Bits and interface blocks from M31 — to the Indian companies designing new chips. They pay a licence fee, drop our block into their design, and ship it to a factory to manufacture. Because the block is built once and licensed many times, the margins are high."

The depth you actually need For each product below: (1) a one-line "what it does," (2) a kitchen-table analogy, (3) the sales angle. Memorise the one-liners and analogies. The deeper notes are there so you're not caught flat-footed if a customer goes one level down.

Where these blocks live on a chip

Green blocks are Analog Bits' core strength; amber are largely M31's interface/foundation domain. Every SoC needs blocks from both sides.

Analog Bits — the core portfolio

PLL (Phase-Locked Loop) — the flagship

What it does: generates and stabilises the clock signal — the rhythmic pulse that synchronises everything on the chip.

Analogy: the conductor of an orchestra. Every musician (circuit block) must play in time. The PLL keeps the beat steady and can speed it up or slow it down precisely.

Sales angle: Every single chip needs a PLL. Vinny said it directly — "every chip needs a PLL for clocking." That makes it the most universal product you sell. Analog Bits' edge: ultra-low jitter (very clean timing), ultra-low power, wide-range, and the "pinless" core-powered design. Realistically only two companies are serious about advanced PLL IP worldwide: Analog Bits and Silicon Creations. That's the "cat-and-mouse" market.

Jitter = tiny timing wobble in the clock. Lower jitter = the chip can run faster and more reliably, which matters enormously for high-speed data and AI chips. Integer vs. fractional PLLs differ in how finely they can dial frequencies. LC PLL (used in Analog Bits' newest "embedded clock LC PLLs") uses an on-chip inductor-capacitor circuit for very low jitter at high frequency — relevant to AI/HPC chips. You don't need to design one; knowing "lower jitter, lower power, wide range, pinless" is enough to sell it.

PVT Sensors (Process, Voltage, Temperature)

What it does: tiny on-chip sensors that continuously monitor temperature, supply voltage, and manufacturing variation across the chip — and detect dangerous voltage "droops" (sudden dips).

Analogy: the fitness tracker / health monitor embedded in the chip — heart rate, temperature, blood pressure — warning the system before something overheats or browns out.

Sales angle: modern AI chips run hot and draw huge, spiky power. Sensors let the system protect itself and optimise power in real time. This is central to Analog Bits' "Intelligent Power Architecture" story and their newest 2nm demos (real-time on-chip power sensing). A strong door-opener for AI/HPC customers.

LDO & droop detectors (power management)

What it does: an LDO (Low-Dropout Regulator) delivers clean, stable voltage right next to where it's needed on the chip; droop detectors catch sudden voltage dips.

Analogy: a local water-pressure regulator placed at each tap, so every part of the house gets steady pressure instead of relying on one distant pump.

Sales angle: Analog Bits' newest growth area. Mahesh's line: "like PLLs maintain clocking stability, we now maintain power integrity in real time." Scalable LDO macros placed next to CPU/AI cores let customers balance power dynamically. This is what's new and exciting in their 2025–2026 announcements — mention it to sound current.

I/O (Input/Output)

What it does: the interface between the chip's internal logic (low voltage) and the outside world (the board, other chips).

Analogy: the doors and loading docks of the chip — how signals get in and out cleanly.

Sales angle: high-performance programmable I/O and chip-to-chip (C2C) connectivity. Increasingly important for chiplets (see below). Analog Bits is presenting on "chiplet scalability" in 2026.

SerDes — present but de-emphasised

Important nuance Vinny flagged On the call you started listing SerDes products from the website. Vinny corrected you: "SerDes is not our forte." Analog Bits has some SerDes available off-the-shelf and will sell it if it fits, but they are not investing further in it. Their claim to fame is clocking (PLLs), then sensors and I/O. Lead with PLLs and sensors, not SerDes. (M31 is the better SerDes story — see below.) Knowing this distinction will impress them, because it shows you listened.

SerDes = Serializer/Deserializer. It converts wide, slow internal data into a fast, narrow stream to send across a few wires, then converts it back at the other end — how chips move huge data over PCIe, between chiplets, or across a board. A PHY is the physical-layer circuit that actually drives those high-speed lanes.

Two bonus terms worth knowing in 2026

Chiplets

Instead of one giant chip, designers now build several smaller "chiplets" and connect them in one package. This needs lots of high-speed chip-to-chip I/O and clocking — directly relevant to Analog Bits' I/O and to M31's interfaces. Analog Bits is presenting on chiplet scalability in 2026.

Mixed-signal

"Mixed-signal" = combining analog (continuous, real-world: voltage, clocks) with digital (1s and 0s) on one chip. Analog Bits is a mixed-signal IP company — their hard part is the analog, which is notoriously difficult and why proven analog IP commands a premium.

Activity: match the customer need to the product

Scenario: A startup building an edge-AI camera SoC tells you their chip runs hot, needs a rock-steady high-speed clock, must talk to a camera module, and they're worried about power dips crashing the AI core. Which products do you propose, and from whom?

  • LDO power management (Analog Bits) — deliver clean, stable power at the core.
  • MIPI C/D-PHY (M31) — to talk to the camera module.

Why India, why now — the one-paragraph macro story

India is making a serious, well-funded push to become a global semiconductor hub. It already trains a huge share of the world's chip-design engineers, and the government is now paying companies to design chips on Indian soil. The manufacturing side (fabs) is being built in Gujarat, Assam and elsewhere, but the fastest-moving, highest-value opportunity is chip design — the fabless startups. Every one of them needs IP. That's your market, and it's expanding from a small base. As Vinny put it: "in India, the sky's the limit today."

Why "trust" is the whole game

Vinny said it three times: "IP business goes on trust. Nothing else." Understand why, so you can speak to it convincingly:

  • The customer is putting your block permanently into their chip. If it fails, their tape-out fails — millions lost.
  • They share confidential roadmaps with you to get the right IP. They must trust you won't leak it (this is also why your Silicon Creations history needs careful handling — Section 8).
  • "First-time-working / silicon-proven" is a trust claim: this has worked in real chips before, so it'll work in yours.
  • In a two-player niche, reputation travels fast. One betrayed confidence ends a career; one great delivery brings referrals for years.

The IP sales cycle, stage by stage

The deal motion. Note the NDA before sharing detailed technical/roadmap info — standard in IP.

What each stage really involves

  • Lead: inbound from IMEC, referrals, trade shows (VLSI / Semicon), LinkedIn outreach. Respond fast — speed signals reliability.
  • Qualify: What are they building? Which process node (5nm? 12nm?)? What's their timeline to tape-out? Are they funded (DLI/VC)? Who decides? This filters real deals from tyre-kickers.
  • Technical fit: the first real meeting. Gather requirements, loop in the IP company's technical/AE support, position the right block. An NDA is usually signed here so the customer can share specifics.
  • Proposal: license fee, any royalty, delivery, support terms. You coordinate with Analog Bits / M31 on pricing.
  • Negotiate: commercial terms, sometimes customisation. This is where "senior" matters.
  • Close & support: contract signed; then you stay close through integration. Happy integration → next chip → referrals. IP is a repeat business.

Running a great first customer meeting

Vinny told you exactly what he looks for: in the first meeting with a prospect, gather what they want and what application they're targeting. You don't need to be deeply technical — you need to listen well and ask the right questions. Have these ready:

  • "What are you building, and what's the end application?" (AI? automotive? IoT? — drives which IP and which performance matters)
  • "Which process node and foundry are you targeting?" (tells you what's silicon-proven and available)
  • "What's your timeline to tape-out?" (urgency + deal stage)
  • "Which blocks are you sourcing externally vs. building in-house?" (finds the opening)
  • "What's most critical — power, performance, area, or time-to-market?" (lets you position the right differentiator)

PLLPhase-Locked Loop — generates/stabilises the chip's clock. Every chip needs one. Analog Bits' flagship.
JitterTiny timing wobble in a clock. Lower jitter = faster, more reliable chip.
PVT sensorOn-chip monitor for Process, Voltage, Temperature — the chip's health tracker.
Droop detectorCatches sudden voltage dips that could crash the chip.
LDOLow-Dropout Regulator — delivers clean, stable voltage right where it's needed. Analog Bits' newest growth area.
I/OInput/Output — how signals get in and out of the chip.
SerDesSerializer/Deserializer — moves data fast over few wires. Not Analog Bits' forte; M31's strength.
PHYThe physical-layer circuit driving a high-speed interface (USB, PCIe, MIPI).
USB / PCIe / MIPI / DDR / UFSStandard interfaces — connectivity, high-speed links, camera/display, memory, storage. M31's interface IP.
Foundation IPThe basic bricks — standard-cell libraries, memory compilers, GPIO/ESD I/O. M31 provides these.
Hard / soft IPHard = fully laid-out, process-validated, drop-in. Soft = flexible design code. Analog/PHY blocks are usually hard.
Mixed-signalCombining analog (clocks, voltage) and digital (1s/0s) on one chip. Analog Bits' domain.
Pinless / core-poweredAnalog Bits' signature — analog blocks placeable anywhere without dedicated supply pins.
ChipletSeveral smaller chips connected in one package instead of one huge chip. Drives demand for I/O + interfaces.
License fee (NRE)Upfront fee to license IP for a design.
RoyaltyPer-chip fee paid as the customer ships volume.
NDANon-Disclosure Agreement — signed so the customer can share confidential design info.
ISMIndia Semiconductor Mission — the government's ~US$10bn semiconductor programme.
DLIDesign Linked Incentive — reimburses up to 50% of design cost; funds your customers.
MeitYMinistry of Electronics & IT — owns ISM and DLI. (Not "METI" — that's Japan.)
C-DACGovernment agency implementing the DLI scheme.
GeMGovernment e-Marketplace — portal for selling to government bodies; SphereLink is on it.
OIPTSMC's Open Innovation Platform — its partner ecosystem. Analog Bits won its 2025 Analog IP Partner of the Year.

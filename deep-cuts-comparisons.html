The architecture deep dive — where the durable difference lives

Silicon Creations: the isolated analog rail

Their PLLs are architected around isolated analog supplies — a dedicated higher-voltage analog rail (their own materials present this as a virtue: excellent supply rejection in noisy SoCs, and it genuinely is well-engineered). The cost: the chip must route and decouple that extra analog rail to every PLL instance, budget the pins or an on-chip supply to feed it, and respect placement constraints. On a small die that's routine; on a reticle-sized AI die or across chiplets it compounds into real floorplan work.

Analog Bits: pinless / core-powered

The patented alternative: the macro cleans and regulates the ordinary core supply internally — no dedicated analog rail, no analog pins. Consequence: place a PLL (or a remote PVT sensor) anywhere on the die like a digital gate. On giant AI dies and chiplet assemblies, placement freedom isn't a convenience — it's the difference between drop-in and redesign. This is the architectural moat, and it extends across the whole macro family.

Say it in one breath "Both houses are excellent at clocking and both are at 2nm now — that's table stakes. The durable differences are two: Analog Bits' pinless architecture removes the analog rail that every competitor macro still needs, and Analog Bits closes the power loop — sense with PVT and droop detectors, respond with LDOs — which Silicon Creations simply doesn't have a product line for. One company sells you a superb clock; the other sells you a stable, observable power-and-clock system."

SECTION 02 · THE COMPARISON

Menta eFPGA, properly

The second name in your current book, and the one you've never had a full brief on. Ten minutes here and you can explain eFPGA to anyone — which quietly proves you can master any IP category, exactly what a CEO hiring a one-person territory wants to see.

What an eFPGA actually is

An embedded FPGA is a patch of reprogrammable logic — look-up tables (LUTs), routing, optionally DSP and memory blocks — delivered as IP and built into a customer's ASIC or SoC. The rest of the chip is fixed forever at tape-out; the eFPGA region can be rewired in the field, any time, by loading a new bitstream.

The analogy that lands "A chip is fired ceramic — its logic is baked and unchangeable. An eFPGA is a patch of soft clay left in the ceramic: after the chip ships, you can still reshape that patch. You pay for the clay in area and speed, so you place it exactly where change is likely — a crypto engine, an evolving protocol, an accelerator you'll tune later."

Why customers buy it

  • Post-silicon adaptability: fix bugs, track evolving standards, and extend product life without a multi-million-dollar respin.
  • Crypto agility: update cryptographic functions after deployment — the reason defence and security buyers love it, and each device's unique bitstream adds security by obfuscation.
  • One chip, many SKUs: the same silicon configured differently per customer or per market.
  • The honest cost: programmable logic is far larger, slower and hungrier per function than hard logic — so it's used surgically, never for the whole chip.

Concepts that stitch it together

Ring vs LC PLLRing oscillator: compact, flexible, more jitter — the everyday clock. LC: an inductor-capacitor tank, ultra-low jitter, bigger — the SerDes-grade clock. Both houses build both
Deskew / ZDBExternal-feedback PLL mode that phase-aligns an output to a far-away clock — "zero-delay buffer" behaviour (a SiCr 16FFC-family feature you can now explain)
LVDS vs CMLTwo differential signalling styles: LVDS = low-power workhorse; CML = faster, hungrier — both are "how fast signals travel on two wires"
Soft vs hard IPSoft = RTL, synthesised into any node (Menta's world; M31 controllers). Hard = fixed layout per node/foundry (AB and SiCr's world — hence "which node?" is always question one). You now sell across both — say so
Embedded-clock LC PLLAB's N3P/N2P line item: LC-quality clocking built to live inside the block it serves — leading-edge vocabulary worth recognising

Self-check

A customer needs SerDes-grade, ultra-low-jitter clocking. Which product class does that point to, from either vendor?
LC PLLs are the low-jitter aristocracy — larger and pricier than ring PLLs, and exactly what multi-gigabit lanes demand. Both houses make them; AB's newest are proven on N2P.

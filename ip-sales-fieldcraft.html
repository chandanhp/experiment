SECTION 02 · SPEAK THE TRADE

Business vocabulary: speak like you've closed deals

You asked for the business terms — OTS, proven, and the rest. Grouped, one breath each. The rule from your other dossiers holds: never use a word you can't define in one breath. Every entry below is that breath.

Commercial terms — the money words

OTS (off-the-shelf)A standard, existing IP product licensed as-is — no custom design work. Faster, cheaper, lower-risk than custom. Vinny's phrase for how Analog Bits sells SerDes: off-the-shelf if it fits, no new investment.
Silicon-provenThe IP has been fabricated on that exact process and measured working in real silicon — not just simulated. The single most load-bearing claim in IP sales.
First-time-workingA vendor's track record of IP working on the first tape-out, no re-spin. Analog Bits' 30-year heritage claim.
Design winA customer commits your IP into a specific chip design. The unit of victory in this business.
SocketA slot in a chip design where an IP block is needed ("the clocking socket"). Each socket is a sale.
License feeThe upfront payment for the right to use IP in a design. The main revenue event.
1st use / 2nd use / subsequent usePricing tiers per design chip: full price for the first chip, discounted for reuse in later chips. Declining reuse pricing is built-in land-and-expand.
NRENon-Recurring Engineering — a one-time fee for custom work (porting to a new node, spec changes). Distinct from the license fee.
RoyaltyPer-unit payment tied to production volume. Common for processors (Arm's engine); less common for analog blocks, which are usually license-led.
M&SMaintenance & Support — an annual fee (typically ~15% of license) for continued support and updates after the included period ends.
Eval licenseLimited license to evaluate IP (models, docs) before committing. A qualification stage, and a trust gesture.
Term sheetA non-binding map of a deal's headline terms, agreed before lawyers draft the full license agreement. Finds deal-breakers early.
PO / Effective DateThe purchase order and the contract start date — deliverable and payment clocks usually start at the later of the two.
Milestone billingPayments tied to delivery events (kit delivered → invoice due), not calendar dates. Also how you align deals to a startup's DLI reimbursement cycle.
Quote validityQuotes expire (30 days is typical) — gentle, legitimate urgency in every deal.
"Subject to definitive agreement"A quote is commercial intent; the legally binding document is the signed IP License Agreement. Two closures: commercial, then legal.

Licensing-legal terms — from the PDF Vinny sent you

Licensor / LicenseeOwner granting rights / party receiving them. You'll represent the licensor's side.
Exclusive vs non-exclusiveExclusive = licensed to one party only (higher fees, often with milestone minimums to keep exclusivity); non-exclusive = licensed to many (the IP-industry norm).
Field of use / TerritoryLimits on where the license applies — by application domain and by geography.
Grant verbsmake, have made, import, use, offer for sale, sell… — each verb is a distinct permission, and omission means prohibition. "Have made" is what allows contract manufacturing.
Royalty-bearing / fully paid-up / royalty-freeOngoing per-unit payments / one lump sum covers everything / no payments (other consideration given).
Reps & warrantiesFormal promises — chiefly that the licensor owns the IP and (where given) that it doesn't infringe third-party rights. Patent noninfringement is the one licensors hesitate on, since patent infringement requires no awareness.
Duty to defend vs duty to indemnifyDefend = pay the legal fight from day one, regardless of outcome. Indemnify = pay damages only after liability. A licensee wants both.
Confidentiality's two duties(1) Don't disclose the other side's information; (2) don't use it beyond this agreement. The second is the one people forget — and the exact discipline you're demonstrating about SiCr.

Deliverables & technical-package terms

FE Design KitFront-end package: LEF footprint (with antenna info), Verilog behavioural model + testbench, Liberty (.lib) timing model, datasheet, application note with integration and production-test guidelines. What the customer's designers integrate against.
BE Design KitBack-end package: everything in FE plus the GDSII layout, Calibre LVS netlist, release notes (including any physical-verification waivers) and the DRC/ANT/LVS/ERC verification logfiles. What goes into the actual chip build.
GDSIIThe final layout database — the IP's physical "artwork" that gets merged into the chip.
PDK / metal stackThe foundry's design-rule kit, and the specific metal-layer configuration a chip uses (e.g. a 9-metal stack). IP must match both — a real qualification question.
Tape-outReleasing the finished design to the foundry. The customer's immovable deadline — and your deal clock.
Corners (TT/SS/FFG), TJProcess/voltage/temperature extremes specs are guaranteed across, and junction temperature. Datasheet fine print that FAEs live in.

Self-check

A customer asks: "Is this PLL OTS and proven on our node?" What are they actually asking?
OTS = standard product, no NRE, fast delivery. Silicon-proven = working measured silicon on that node. Together they mean: minimum risk, minimum time — the two things every tape-out-driven customer is really buying.

PJ vs LTJ — the crash card

PJ — Period Jitter

How much each clock period varies from the next. Matters for digital timing closure (STA) — the shortest period that will ever occur sets your timing margin. Usually specified peak-to-peak (or one-sided peak), sometimes as % of period.

LTJ — Long-Term (Phase) Jitter

How far a clock edge drifts from where it should be after many cycles — errors accumulate, because phase is the integral of frequency. Matters for ADC/DAC sampling, SerDes, RF — a mistimed sample is a wrong sample. Usually specified RMS or as integrated phase noise.

The subtlety worth quoting: a clock can have small PJ and still large LTJ. And deterministic jitter comes mostly from supply noise — which is why power integrity and clock quality are the same conversation. (That last sentence is an Analog Bits thesis, hiding inside a Silicon Creations form.)

Crash notes: the licensing PDF in one card

  • The frame: licensing = renting IP, not selling it. Licensor grants; licensee receives. Ownership never moves — only specified permissions do.
  • Exclusive vs non-exclusive: exclusive costs more and usually carries milestone minimums so the licensee can't shelve the technology; non-exclusive is the IP-industry norm.
  • Grant verbs are the contract: make, have made, import, use, offer for sale, sell — each is a separate permission, and a missing verb is a prohibition. No "have made" = no contract manufacturing.
  • Payment shapes: royalty-bearing (per unit), fully paid-up (one lump sum), royalty-free (other consideration). Plus field-of-use and territory to slice the rights.
  • Reps & warranties: ownership is standard; patent noninfringement is the one licensors resist — patent infringement requires no awareness, so nobody can promise it absolutely.
  • Defend ≠ indemnify: defend = pay the legal fight from day one regardless of outcome; indemnify = pay damages after liability. A licensee wants both; missing either leaves them exposed.
  • Confidentiality has two duties: don't disclose, and don't use beyond the agreement — the forgotten second duty that can "infect" unrelated projects.
  • Term sheet: the non-binding one-pager that finds deal-breakers before lawyers bill hours. The quote you studied says "subject to a mutually acceptable IP license agreement" — that's this concept, live.

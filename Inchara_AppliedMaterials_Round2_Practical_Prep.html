What round 1 taught us — and the mindset shift for round 2

They asked what you knew about the company and its products, then hit you with a live scenario: you committed an order on an estimate, the need disappeared — and when you said "I'll use the material elsewhere," they blocked it: "I don't want it anywhere. Now what?"

Why they blocked your answer: "use it elsewhere" is a planning answer. They were testing whether you know the buyer's commercial toolkit — the levers you pull when material genuinely cannot be consumed: reschedule, cancellation terms, liability negotiation, settlement.

The interviewer wasn't being difficult. In semiconductor equipment, demand swings hard and orders get cancelled with vendors mid-production. Handling that commercially is a daily part of this exact job — the JD even names the tools: pull-ins, pushouts, planner orders.

The mindset for tomorrow

  • Answer in ladders, not one-liners. For every scenario: verify the situation → check the paperwork → try the cheapest lever first → escalate the levers → document and prevent. Interviewers keep pushing until your ladder runs out — so bring a tall ladder.
  • When they block a step, climb to the next one calmly. "If that's not possible, then my next step is…" That composure under pushback IS the test.
  • Use their vocabulary. Pushout, pull-in, cancellation liability, exception messages, line-down. It signals you're already one of them.
  • It's okay to end a ladder with escalation. The JD says negotiation "under supervision" — saying "at that point I'd bring my manager in with a clear summary of options and costs" is a correct final rung, not a weakness.

File 03 · MRP Crash Course

Speak MRP — the vocabulary this JD is written in

The JD says: "review MRP regularly and execute pull-ins, pushouts and planner orders." Learn these five terms and the daily loop, and you'll sound like you've done this job before.

The five terms

TermMeaning
MRPMaterial Requirements Planning — the system engine that compares demand (what production needs, and when) against supply (stock + open POs) and tells the buyer what action to take.
Planned / planner orderA system-generated proposal to buy: "you'll be short of part X on date Y — order Z quantity." The buyer converts it into a requisition/PO after sanity-checking quantity, date and source.
Pull-inDemand moved earlier → ask the supplier to deliver an existing order sooner than the PO date. Tools: check vendor's build status, partial shipments, premium freight.
PushoutDemand moved later → reschedule the delivery to a later date. The cheapest way to avoid excess inventory — always preferred over cancelling.
CancelDemand disappeared → cancel the order, subject to the PO's cancellation terms and negotiation of any cancellation liability (see Scenario 1).

The buyer's daily MRP loop — describe this as your workflow

  1. Run/review MRP exceptions. The system flags action messages on your parts: expedite (pull-in), postpone (pushout), cancel, or new planned orders.
  2. Sanity-check before acting. Is the demand change real and stable, or noise? Check with planning before disturbing a supplier — flip-flopping dates destroys supplier trust.
  3. Execute the actions. Convert planned orders to POs; send reschedule requests; confirm new dates with suppliers in writing.
  4. Keep the data honest. Update PO dates, lead times and costs in the system so the next MRP run reflects reality — the JD calls this data integrity.
  5. Follow up and close. Track confirmations, chase deliveries at risk, close completed POs.

Supporting vocabulary — one line each

  • Lead time — order-to-delivery time; the single most important number to keep accurate in the system.
  • Safety stock — buffer held against variability; more buffer = fewer fires but more cash tied up.
  • MOQ / SPQ — minimum order quantity / standard pack quantity a supplier will accept; often forces buying more than MRP asks.
  • Line-down — production stopped for want of a part; the emergency that outranks everything.
  • Cancellation liability — what you owe a supplier for work already done when you cancel; depends on how far they've progressed (raw material → work-in-progress → finished goods) and whether the part is custom or standard.
  • Open order book — all your live POs; a clean, accurate book is the mark of a disciplined buyer.
File 04 · The Scenario Bank

Practical questions — with the answer ladder for each

This is the round. Each scenario shows what they're testing, the trap that catches weaker candidates, and the step-by-step ladder to climb — so when they block one step, you calmly take the next.

The round-1 question — done right this time
1 · "You ordered against an estimate. The requirement is gone. You can't use the material anywhere else. Now what?"
Testing: cancellation liability & commercial levers
The trapOperational answers — "use it elsewhere," "keep it in stock," "return it" — they'll block each one. They want the commercial ladder.
  1. Verify the demand change first. Is it cancelled or just delayed? Confirm with planning before touching the supplier — because the answer decides everything.
  2. If delayed → negotiate a pushout, not a cancellation. Reschedule delivery to when demand returns. Cheapest lever; supplier keeps the order, we avoid dead stock.
  3. Check the PO / contract terms. What do our cancellation clauses say? What notice window? Is the item standard-catalog or custom / built-to-print? That sets my negotiating position.
  4. Freeze further work. Ask the supplier to stop immediately — every day they keep building increases the liability.
  5. Negotiate liability by stage of completion. Raw material only? Ask them to return it to their supplier or divert to other customers — minimal cost. Work-in-progress? Negotiate actual costs incurred, not full price. Finished goods, standard part? They can resell — small or no liability. Finished, custom part? That's the worst case — negotiate a settlement on cost, not price, with no margin on cancelled work.
  6. Offer creative settlements before cash. Partial acceptance now, balance cancelled; supplier holds stock for a defined window in case demand returns; a credit note adjusted against future orders; a reduced restocking fee. Vendors prefer future business over a fight.
  7. Escalate and document honestly. Summarise the options and costs for my manager, book the agreed liability transparently with finance, and keep records clean — no hiding a bad outcome.
  8. Prevent the repeat. Review why the estimate missed; order in smaller lots against a blanket agreement; add reschedule windows and liability caps to PO terms; agree liability matrices with regular suppliers up front.
The closing line"So my sequence is: verify, prefer a pushout, stop work, then negotiate the liability down based on how far they've built and what the terms say — and settle creatively, protecting the relationship. And afterwards, fix the process so the exposure is smaller next time."
MRP & delivery scenarios
2 · "Production suddenly needs the material three weeks earlier than the PO date. What do you do?"
Testing: pull-in / expedite execution
  1. Confirm the real need-date and quantity with planning — expedites are expensive; make sure it's real.
  2. Call the supplier and check build status. Where is my order — raw material, in production, finished? That tells me what's physically possible.
  3. Ask for a partial pull-in. Ship whatever is ready now, balance on the original date — often enough to keep the line running.
  4. Trade for speed. Overtime, priority slotting, premium/air freight — and weigh that cost against the cost of a line-down, with my manager for approval.
  5. Bridge from alternates if needed — an approved second source or nearby stock for a small bridge quantity.
  6. Update the system and communicate — new dates in the PO, honest ETA to production, daily tracking until it lands.
3 · "MRP shows the requirement moved out by two months, but the vendor is about to ship. Handle it."
Testing: pushout discipline
The trapSaying "cancel it." A pushout almost always beats a cancellation — and jumping to cancel signals you don't know the cost difference.
  1. Check the signal is stable — one MRP run can be noise; confirm with planning before disturbing the supplier.
  2. Request the reschedule before it ships — once it's on a truck, options shrink. Ask to hold and deliver at the new date.
  3. Negotiate holding terms if they push back — brief storage at their end, or split delivery (small qty now, rest later) to share the burden.
  4. Watch the payment terms — if payment triggers on the original date, align it to the new delivery.
  5. Update PO dates immediately so the next MRP run is honest, and note the change history.
4 · "A supplier confirmed delivery, then went quiet. The line goes down in ten days. Walk me through it."
Testing: escalation & shortage management
  1. Get the truth today — call, not email; get exact status and a firm recovery date; ask what's blocking them.
  2. Escalate inside the supplier — if my contact stalls, go to their sales manager / plant head; a confirmed-then-missed commitment justifies it.
  3. Open parallel options immediately — partial shipment, premium freight, approved alternate source for a bridge quantity. Don't wait for the vendor to fail twice.
  4. Tell production early with honest dates — they can resequence builds around the gap. Bad news early is manageable; bad news late is a crisis.
  5. Escalate internally with facts — impact date, options, costs — so my manager can back the recovery plan.
  6. Afterwards: score it — record the miss in the supplier's delivery performance, hold a corrective-action conversation.
Key line"My rule is: production never gets surprised. The moment a date is at risk, they know, with my recovery plan attached."
5 · "Your vendor delivered excess / you discover excess inventory on your parts. What now?"
Testing: inventory ownership
  1. Find the root cause first — over-ordering, demand drop, duplicate PO, MOQ forcing big lots? The cause decides the fix.
  2. Stop the inflow — push out or cancel remaining open POs on that part (cheapest lever, costs nothing).
  3. Work the stock down — align with planning to consume it first before any new buys.
  4. Negotiate with the supplier — return with a restocking fee, or buy-back credit against future orders.
  5. Fix the setting — correct the MOQ/lot size/safety stock parameter that caused it, so it doesn't rebuild.
Negotiation & supplier scenarios
6 · "A supplier demands a 15% price increase citing raw material costs. Respond."
Testing: negotiation method
  1. Never accept on the call. Acknowledge, ask for the request in writing with a cost breakdown.
  2. Validate the claim — which cost element rose, by how much? Check commodity indices; raw material is usually only part of their cost, so a 15% input rise doesn't justify 15% on price.
  3. Counter proportionally — offer the validated portion only, phased in, with a review date to roll it back if inputs fall.
  4. Trade, don't just resist — longer commitment or better payment terms in exchange for a smaller increase.
  5. Benchmark quietly — refresh RFQs with alternates so I negotiate from knowledge, not hope.
  6. Escalate per policy — significant increases go to my manager with my analysis and recommendation.
7 · "Incoming inspection rejects a delivered lot. What's your process?"
Testing: quality + P2P discipline
  1. Quarantine the lot and gather the evidence — inspection report, photos, spec deviation.
  2. Notify the supplier formally the same day with the data; raise the rejection/return in the system.
  3. Solve supply first — replacement on expedite, or sorting/rework if faster and acceptable to quality.
  4. Protect the money — hold payment on the rejected quantity (the 3-way match protects us), raise a debit/credit note per process.
  5. Close the loop — corrective action from the supplier, and log it in their quality score.
8 · "You're dependent on a single source and they know it. How do you manage that?"
Testing: risk thinking
  1. Short term — protect supply, not ego. Keep the relationship professional, forecast openly with them, hold a little more safety stock on their parts.
  2. Contract the risk down — lock pricing and lead times in an agreement while goodwill exists.
  3. Medium term — build the alternative — work with engineering to qualify a second source, even for partial volume; the credible existence of an alternate changes negotiations.
  4. Split volume when qualified — e.g. 70/30 — to keep both suppliers honest.
9 · "Engineering insists on one specific brand; a cheaper equivalent exists. What do you do?"
Testing: cross-functional maturity
  1. Understand before pushing — ask engineering what's technically critical; sometimes the brand lock is real (qualification, reliability), sometimes habit.
  2. Bring data, not opinion — cost delta, availability, lead-time comparison of the alternative.
  3. Propose a controlled evaluation — sample qualification of the alternate with engineering owning the technical verdict.
  4. Accept the outcome — if it fails qualification, the premium is justified; if it passes, we've saved money with engineering on-side. I never override a technical owner unilaterally.
Judgement, compliance & prioritisation
10 · "It's urgent, but the PO approval is stuck. Do you tell the vendor to start anyway?"
Testing: compliance under pressure — a trap question
The trapSaying yes to look action-oriented. At a US-listed MNC with strict controls, verbally committing spend without an approved PO is exactly what they screen against.
Say this"No — I don't commit company spend without an approved PO; that's a control for good reason. What I do is attack the approval: flag the urgency to the approvers directly, get my manager to fast-track it, and use the company's emergency-purchase process if one exists. I'll prepare everything so the order releases the minute it's approved — but the sequence stays: approval first, commitment after."
11 · "A vendor offers you a personal gift / favour during a negotiation. What do you do?"
Testing: ethics — instant-fail if fumbled
Say this"I politely decline and disclose it to my manager per the company's gift and ethics policy. Anything beyond a token courtesy compromises my objectivity as a buyer — and my credibility is the one thing I can't buy back. I'd keep the negotiation strictly on merit."

Short, immediate, no hedging. This one is pass/fail.

12 · "You have 60 open POs and ten are slipping. How do you prioritise your day?"
Testing: workload judgement
  1. Rank by production impact, not noise — which slips actually threaten a build or a line-down, and when is the true need-date?
  2. Work the critical few hard — direct calls, recovery plans, escalation on the ones that stop production.
  3. Batch the rest — standard follow-ups and reschedules in a disciplined daily/weekly rhythm driven by MRP exception messages.
  4. Keep the book clean — close completed POs, correct dates, so tomorrow's exception list is truth, not clutter.
13 · "How do you decide how much to order?"
Testing: planning fundamentals

Start from the MRP requirement (demand net of stock and open orders), then adjust for reality: lead time (order early enough), safety stock policy, supplier MOQ/SPQ and lot sizes, and price breaks vs inventory cost — a quantity discount is only a saving if the extra stock doesn't sit for a year. If my judgement says the system quantity looks wrong, I check with planning rather than blindly ordering.

14 · "A supplier ships early / ships more than ordered. Do you accept it?"
Testing: PO discipline detail

Check the company's receiving tolerance policy first — many allow a small % over-shipment or a few days early. Within tolerance: accept, note it. Outside tolerance: it ties up cash and space and can distort MRP — so return or hold, per policy, and correct the behaviour with the supplier. Early/over shipping is often a supplier pushing their inventory onto us; a good buyer notices the pattern.

15 · "Tell me about a real expedite or shortage you handled at your current job."
Testing: is your experience real

Have ONE true story sharp and ready in STAR form — a real material, a real deadline, what you personally did (calls, partial shipment, escalation, alternate), and the outcome (line kept running / delivery recovered). Then bridge:

Bridge line"That was on packaging materials at Alternicq — the same discipline this JD calls shortage management and expediting, applied to more complex parts here."

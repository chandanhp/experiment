The four forces driving the market — with real examples

1 · AI is reshaping silicon demand

Hyperscalers and AI startups are building custom silicon, and every one of those chips needs clocking, power and interface IP. Real example to cite: Analog Bits co-published with Cerebras — the wafer-scale AI company — on on-die power management for SoCs and chiplets. That's the AI thesis in one sentence: monster AI chips need real-time power sensing and delivery, which is exactly the portfolio Analog Bits has built.

2 · Chiplets change what IP gets bought

Instead of one giant die, designs are splitting into chiplets connected by die-to-die links (the UCIe standard). This multiplies demand for interface IP, I/O, and per-die clocking/sensing. Real example: UCIe adoption is growing fast, and Analog Bits presents on chiplet scalability; its Cerebras paper is literally titled for "SoCs and Chiplets."

3 · RISC-V and silicon sovereignty

Countries and companies want architectural independence; RISC-V (the open processor architecture) is the vehicle. Real example: a large share of India's DLI startups build on RISC-V — and India's 1-TOPS student program aims at full RISC-V SoC tape-outs. Every RISC-V SoC still needs third-party analog IP: the processor may be open, the PLL never is.

4 · Royalty economics

The IP business model = licence fee upfront + royalty per chip shipped. Royalties are the majority of industry revenue (~55–70%), which is why IP companies compound: win the socket once, earn for the life of the product. This is the "60–70% gross margin" business Vinny described.

The 30-second global-market answer, assembled "Silicon IP is roughly an eight-billion-dollar market growing high-single-digits, feeding a $600-billion chip industry. Arm dominates processor IP, Synopsys and Cadence bundle broad IP with their EDA, and then there's the specialist layer where we play — precision analog and mixed-signal that the giants don't do best. The growth drivers right now are AI silicon, chiplets, and sovereignty programs like India's — and all three pull demand toward exactly what Analog Bits sells: clocking, sensing, and power for big, hot, modular chips. Cerebras co-publishing with Analog Bits on on-die power management is the perfect example."

Win the socket once, earn per chip for the product's life. That compounding is the economic heart of the IP business.

SECTION 05 · TECHNICAL FLUENCY

Nodes & the IP landscape

"Nodes, IPs" was on Vinny's list. You need conversational fluency: what a node is, which nodes matter for which customers, and how the IP world is categorised.

Process nodes in one map

Node bandExamplesTypical useIndia relevance
Leading edge2nm (N2/N2P), 3nmFlagship AI, mobile, HPCQualcomm India 2nm tape-out; Analog Bits demos on N2P — your premium story
Advanced5nm, 7nm (FinFET)High-end SoCs, automotive computeWhere ambitious Indian startups aim next; M31's advanced PHYs live here

Two vocabulary upgrades that signal fluency: below ~16nm transistors are FinFET (3D fins) and at 2nm the industry moves to GAA (gate-all-around); and node names are marketing labels more than literal dimensions — what matters commercially is which node your customer is taping out on, and whether your IP is silicon-proven there.

The killer question you should always ask a customer "Which node and which foundry?" — because IP is not portable free-of-charge. A PLL proven on TSMC 28nm must be ported and re-proven for Samsung 14nm. This is why portfolio breadth across nodes/foundries (Analog Bits: 0.35µm→2nm, all major foundries) is a real moat, and why the question instantly marks you as someone who knows the business.

The IP taxonomy — how the industry categorises what you sell

By function

  • Processor IP — CPU/GPU/NPU cores (Arm, RISC-V). ~40% of licensing.
  • Interface IP — USB, PCIe, MIPI, DDR, SerDes (M31's world). ~25%.
  • Analog/mixed-signal IP — PLLs, sensors, LDOs, ADCs (Analog Bits' and Silicon Creations' world).
  • Foundation IP — standard cells, memory compilers, GPIO (M31).
  • Memory & Security IP — controllers, root-of-trust.

By delivery

  • Soft IP — RTL code; flexible, customer implements; portable across nodes.
  • Hard IP — finished physical layout, proven on a specific node/foundry; what analog and PHY blocks must be.
  • Key consequence: hard analog IP is node-locked → porting is real work → silicon-proven-on-your-node is the whole sales argument.

Self-check

A customer says they're taping out on Samsung 14nm. Your Silicon Creations experience is mostly TSMC. What's the FIRST thing you check?
Hard IP is locked to node+foundry. Availability on the customer's target process is question zero — everything else follows.

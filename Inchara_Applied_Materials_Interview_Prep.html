"I'm a procurement professional with about a year and a half of hands-on buying experience. 04 / TECHNICAL CORE

SAP MM, explained the way they'll ask it

Learn the concepts, not just definitions — you should be able to explain each in plain language and connect it to what you already do. This is the heart of the technical screen.

What is SAP MM?

SAP MM (Materials Management) is the SAP module that handles procurement and inventory — material planning, purchasing, inventory management, vendor valuation and invoice verification. Its job is to make sure the right materials are available in the right quantity, at the right time and cost, without shortages or excess. It's the backbone of the logistics area and connects to other modules like PP (Production Planning), SD (Sales & Distribution) and FI (Finance).

The Procure-to-Pay (P2P) cycle — know this cold

This is the single most important thing to be fluent in. Walk through it as a sequence:

  1. Determine requirement — a need for material is identified (manually or via MRP).
  2. Purchase Requisition (PR) — an internal document requesting the purchase.
  3. Source determination / RFQ — find suppliers; send Requests for Quotation, compare quotes.
  4. Vendor selection & PO — choose the vendor; issue the Purchase Order, a binding external document.
  5. Goods Receipt (GR) — material arrives; you record receipt and stock updates.
  6. Invoice Verification (IR) — match invoice against PO and GR (the 3-way match).
  7. Payment — finance releases payment to the vendor.

Tip: when you describe this, add "— and this is exactly what I do daily on D365," so it lands as experience, not theory.

Master data — the three you must name

Master recordWhat it holds
Material MasterAll data about a material — description, unit of measure, valuation, plant/storage views. The central reference for every transaction.
Vendor MasterAll data about a supplier — address, payment terms, banking, purchasing org data.
Purchasing Info RecordLinks a specific material to a specific vendor — price, conditions, lead time. Drives faster, accurate POs.

Bonus: Source List (which vendors are allowed/preferred for a material) and Conditions/Pricing records often come up too.

The org structure — how SAP is built

A frequent intermediate question. Know the hierarchy top to bottom:

LevelMeaning
ClientTop level — the whole SAP system / corporate group.
Company CodeA legal entity for which you produce financial statements.
PlantA manufacturing or operational site; where MRP and inventory live.
Storage LocationWhere stock physically sits within a plant.
Purchasing OrganisationUnit responsible for procurement; negotiates terms with vendors.
Purchasing GroupA buyer or group of buyers responsible for certain materials.

Key concepts to have ready

  • PR vs PO: PR is internal (a request); PO is external and legally binding (sent to the vendor).
  • 3-Way Match: Invoice verification matches PO ↔ Goods Receipt ↔ Invoice on quantity and price before payment — prevents overpayment and fraud.
  • MRP vs CBP: MRP (Material Requirements Planning) is consumption- and demand-driven; CBP (Consumption-Based Planning) plans purely from past consumption / reorder points.
  • Movement types: codes that classify stock movements — e.g. 101 goods receipt against PO, 102 reversal of GR, 201 goods issue to cost centre, 261 goods issue to order, 561 initial stock entry.
  • Special procurement / stocks: consignment (vendor's stock at your site, paid on use), subcontracting (you send components, vendor returns finished goods), stock transfer (between plants).
  • Release strategy: approval workflow — POs/PRs above certain values need authorised sign-off before release.
  • Account determination: how MM automatically posts to the right GL accounts in FI when goods/invoices are received.
  • Pricing procedure / conditions: how the net price on a PO is built up — gross price, discounts, freight, taxes — via condition types.
  • Document types: standard PO (NB), stock transport order, contract, scheduling agreement.

Outline agreements — contracts vs scheduling agreements

An outline agreement is a longer-term arrangement with a vendor. A contract commits to a total quantity or value over time, released via individual POs. A scheduling agreement sets fixed delivery dates/quantities via a delivery schedule — common in production environments to keep a steady flow of material. Mentioning these signals you understand procurement beyond one-off POs.

06 / SAP MM Q&A BANK

Technical Q&A — the questions interviewers actually ask

Tap each to reveal a model answer. Where it's a pure-SAP question you may not have lived, the answer shows how to demonstrate concept knowledge honestly. Read the "why" notes — follow-ups usually probe exactly there.

Fundamentals
Walk me through the procurement cycle end to end.

"It starts with a requirement, raised as a purchase requisition — manually or auto-generated by MRP. We determine the source, send RFQs and compare quotations, then select a vendor and raise a purchase order. When the goods arrive we do a goods receipt, which updates stock. Then invoice verification does a three-way match between PO, goods receipt and invoice. Once that clears, finance releases payment. I run this exact cycle every day on D365."

Why this works This is the foundational question. A clean, ordered walkthrough — with the closing line tying it to real experience — signals competence in 20 seconds.
What's the difference between a purchase requisition and a purchase order?

"A purchase requisition is an internal document — it's a request to buy something, raised within the company, that hasn't gone to a vendor yet. A purchase order is the external, legally binding document we send to the chosen vendor confirming what we're buying, the quantity, price, delivery date and terms. A PR typically becomes a PO once the source and price are finalised."

Why this works Internal-vs-external is the crisp distinction they want. The "PR becomes a PO" link shows you understand the flow, not just two definitions.
What are the master data records in SAP MM?

"The three core ones are the material master, which holds everything about a material; the vendor master, which holds supplier details; and the purchasing info record, which links a specific material to a specific vendor with agreed price, lead time and conditions. There's also the source list, which controls which vendors are approved for a material, and condition records for pricing."

Why this works Naming all three core records plus a bonus (source list) shows depth. The info record especially — many juniors forget it.
What is a goods receipt, and what happens in the system when you post one?

"A goods receipt records that material delivered against a PO has physically arrived. When you post it — in SAP that's MIGO — stock quantity increases, a material document and an accounting document are created, the stock value updates, and it sets up the goods-receipt side of the three-way match for invoice verification. It also updates the PO history."

Why this works Going beyond "it means goods arrived" to the system effects (material doc + accounting doc + stock update) is what separates a trained answer from a guess.
Explain the three-way match.

"It's the control in invoice verification where the system matches three documents before approving payment: the purchase order, the goods receipt, and the vendor's invoice — checking that quantity and price agree across all three. If they match, payment is released; if there's a discrepancy, it's blocked for review. It prevents overpayment, duplicate payment and fraud."

Why this works Naming all three documents and the purpose (control / fraud prevention) is exactly the complete answer. This comes up very often.
Intermediate
What is the difference between MRP and consumption-based planning?

"MRP is demand-driven — it plans procurement based on actual and forecast requirements, like production orders and sales. Consumption-based planning is driven by historical consumption — it triggers procurement using reorder points or forecasts from past usage, without a direct link to specific demand. CBP suits low-value or steady-use materials; MRP suits materials tied directly to production."

Why this works Most candidates can define MRP but stumble on CBP. The "when to use which" line shows applied understanding.
What is a purchasing info record and why is it useful?

"It's master data that links a material to a vendor and stores the agreed price, conditions, lead time and other terms. It's useful because when you create a PO for that material-vendor combination, the system can pull the price and conditions automatically — it speeds up PO creation, improves accuracy, and gives you price history for negotiation."

Why this works The "price history for negotiation" point connects a technical object to the buyer's real job — exactly the kind of link interviewers reward.
What is consignment stock? Subcontracting?

"In consignment, the vendor's stock physically sits at our location but still belongs to the vendor — we only pay when we consume it. It reduces our inventory holding cost. Subcontracting is where we send components to a vendor who processes or assembles them and returns the finished item — we're paying for the service plus any added material. Both are special procurement types in SAP MM."

Why this works These two are classic "special stock" interview favourites. Crisp definitions with the business benefit (consignment reduces holding cost) signal real understanding.
What is a release strategy / release procedure?

"It's the approval workflow for purchasing documents. Based on criteria like value, document type or material group, a PR or PO needs sign-off from one or more authorised people before it can be released and processed. It enforces spending controls and segregation of duties — a buyer can't approve their own high-value PO."

Why this works Mentioning "segregation of duties" and "spending control" shows you understand the governance reason, not just the mechanics — valuable at a large MNC with strict controls.
What are movement types? Give examples.

"Movement types are three-digit codes that classify every goods movement so the system knows how to update stock and accounting. Common ones: 101 is goods receipt against a PO, 102 reverses it, 201 is goods issue to a cost centre, 261 is goods issue to a production order, and 561 is initial stock upload. The movement type drives the account postings behind the scenes."

Why this works Even two or three correct codes (101, 201, 561) prove hands-on familiarity. The "drives account postings" line shows you understand why they exist.
How does SAP MM integrate with FI and PP?

"With FI (Finance): when we post a goods receipt or invoice, MM automatically creates accounting documents through account determination — so procurement updates the financials in real time. With PP (Production Planning): MRP in PP generates the material requirements that become purchase requisitions in MM, and the materials we procure feed production orders. MM sits between planning and finance."

Why this works Integration questions test whether you see the bigger system. "MM sits between planning and finance" is a memorable, accurate summary.
What is the difference between a contract and a scheduling agreement?

"Both are outline agreements — longer-term deals with a vendor. A contract commits to a total quantity or value over a period, and you release individual POs against it as needed. A scheduling agreement goes further by fixing delivery dates and quantities through a delivery schedule, so material flows in on a planned cadence — which is common in production supply."

Why this works Knowing outline agreements puts you above basic-PO-only candidates and fits a production-purchasing role like this one.
Honesty mode — when you don't know

If you hit a deep-config question you genuinely don't know (e.g. "how do you configure a pricing procedure?"), don't bluff. Say: "I haven't done that configuration myself — my SAP exposure is through training, and my live config work has been on D365. But conceptually I understand what a pricing procedure does: it builds the net price from condition types like base price, discounts, freight and tax. I'd be able to learn the SAP configuration quickly because I understand the logic." Honesty + demonstrated concept knowledge beats a wrong bluff every time.

07 / SCENARIO QUESTIONS

Situational questions — show how you think

The JD lists shortage management, expedite, vendor analysis and cost savings. Expect "what would you do if…" questions. Answer with a clear, logical process — they're testing judgement, not a single right answer.

A critical vendor will miss a delivery and it threatens production. What do you do?
Shortage / expedite — JD priority
  1. Confirm the exact shortfall and the revised date directly with the vendor.
  2. Assess production impact with planning/stores — how many days of buffer stock exist?
  3. Expedite: push the vendor for partial/early shipment, faster freight, or a split delivery.
  4. Check alternate/approved sources (source list) for emergency supply.
  5. Communicate transparently to production, stores and finance so they can re-plan.
  6. Update the system (PO dates, lead time) so data integrity holds.
  7. After the fire's out, review why it happened — vendor reliability, lead-time accuracy — to prevent a repeat.
Why this works A structured "assess → act → communicate → prevent" answer shows mature judgement. The last step (root-cause review) is what senior buyers do and most juniors forget — it elevates the whole answer. The JD literally names "shortage management — expedite process" and "data integrity," so you're hitting their words.
Avoid Jumping straight to "find another vendor." Show you assess impact and communicate first — panic-sourcing is a junior reflex.
How do you approach a vendor negotiation to get a better price?
Negotiation & cost saving
  • Do the homework: market price, past purchase price, cost breakup (raw material + conversion + freight).
  • Use volume leverage — commit to higher volume or a longer contract for a better rate.
  • Get multiple quotes (RFQ) to create competition and a benchmark.
  • Negotiate the total cost — payment terms, freight, packaging — not just unit price.
  • Protect quality and delivery reliability — cheapest isn't always lowest total cost.
  • Keep the relationship constructive — you'll need this vendor again.
Why this works "Total cost, not unit price" and "do the homework first" are the marks of a real negotiator. Mentioning the relationship shows maturity — you're not just squeezing.
Avoid Implying negotiation = bullying the vendor down. Best buyers create value, not just pressure.

Tie it to your real win — see your STAR stories in the Behavioral section.

How do you do a vendor comparison / vendor analysis?
Vendor analysis — JD priority

Explain you score vendors on more than price: price, quality, delivery reliability, lead time, payment terms, capacity, certifications and past performance. Build a comparison matrix, weight the criteria by what matters for that material, and pick on total value. Then track performance over time, not just at quoting.

Why this works The weighted-matrix approach shows structured, defensible decision-making — exactly what "vendor analysis, making comparisons" in the JD is probing.
How would you reduce procurement cost / inventory?
You've actually done this
  • Budget-vs-actual analysis to spot overspend (your real work).
  • Vendor consolidation and volume negotiation.
  • Right-sizing order quantities and reorder points to cut excess inventory.
  • Reducing dead/slow-moving stock through data analysis.
  • Improving lead-time accuracy so you hold less safety stock.
  • Better payment terms to improve working capital.
Why this works You've genuinely done budget-vs-actual and inventory reduction — lead with those as lived experience, then add the others as breadth. Real beats theoretical every time.
What is "Built to Print" and OEM commodity purchasing? (from the JD)
Know the term even if it's new

Built-to-Print means buying parts made exactly to the customer's own drawings/specifications — the supplier manufactures to your print, so you manage the spec, tooling and quality tightly. OEM commodities are components sourced from original-equipment manufacturers. Be honest if it's new to you:

"I haven't bought built-to-print parts directly — that's newer to me — but I understand the concept: purchasing to a fixed engineering spec where I own the drawing, tooling and quality conformance. My materials background means I'm comfortable working from technical specs, so I'd pick this up quickly."

Why this works The JD specifically asks for "Built to Print and OEM commodities" experience. Showing you know what the term means — even without direct experience — proves you did your homework and can speak their language.
A vendor's invoice doesn't match the PO. What do you do?
Process judgement

"First I'd identify whether the mismatch is in quantity or price, and check it against the goods receipt — the three-way match. If it's a genuine error, I'd hold the invoice, raise it with the vendor, and get a corrected invoice or credit note. If it's a legitimate change we agreed, I'd update the PO so the documents reconcile. I wouldn't release payment until all three documents agree, to protect against overpayment."

Why this works Ties directly to the three-way match concept and shows financial discipline — exactly the "data integrity" the JD calls out.
How do you ensure data integrity in procurement systems? (JD wording)
Direct JD phrase — they will ask

"By keeping master data accurate — correct lead times, prices and vendor details — because everything downstream relies on it. I validate POs against agreed terms before release, reconcile goods receipts and invoices carefully, and update the system promptly when dates or quantities change. Clean, current data is what lets planning and finance trust the numbers."

Why this works The JD explicitly says "responsible for procurement business systems data input and integrity such as lead time and cost." Echoing that responsibility back, with concrete habits, shows you read and understood the role.
08 / THE COMPANY

Applied Materials & semiconductors — enough to sound informed

You don't need to be an engineer. You need to show you researched them and understand why procurement matters in this industry. A few crisp talking points are plenty.

Who they are

Applied Materials is the world's largest supplier of equipment and materials-engineering solutions used to manufacture semiconductor chips and advanced displays. Their machines modify materials at the atomic scale so chipmakers can build the processors, memory and displays inside phones, computers, cars, and AI data centres. Tagline: "Make Possible." They're US-headquartered (Santa Clara, California) with a major presence in India.

Semiconductor basics — the 90-second version

  • A semiconductor / chip is the "brain" of electronics — tiny circuits etched onto silicon wafers.
  • Chips are built on round silicon wafers through hundreds of process steps: deposition, etching, ion implantation, polishing.
  • Applied Materials doesn't make the chips — it makes the equipment that fabs (factories like TSMC, Intel, Samsung) use to make chips. They're a "picks and shovels" supplier to the whole industry.
  • Demand is booming because of AI, data centres, EVs and smartphones — more compute everywhere means more chips, means more equipment.

Words you can drop to sound fluent

Wafer fab Deposition / etch Atomic-scale engineering OEM commodities Built-to-print Global supply chain Lead-time critical

Why procurement matters here (your angle)

Connect the industry back to your role so it doesn't feel like trivia:

"Because Applied Materials builds highly complex, precision equipment, procurement is mission-critical — a single delayed or off-spec component can hold up a multi-million-dollar tool. That's why the role emphasises lead-time and cost data integrity, shortage management and supplier reliability. That precision and accountability is exactly what excites me about moving here from packaging procurement."

If asked "what do you know about us?" — give the "who they are" paragraph, then the procurement angle. That combination impresses.

09 / BEHAVIORAL

Behavioral questions — build these STAR stories now

Use the STAR structure: Situation → Task → Action → Result. Prepare 3–4 real stories from Alternicq before Friday; you can reuse them across many questions. Fill in real numbers where you can — they're the difference between forgettable and convincing.

Story 1 — A cost saving (your strongest)

S/T
A material's cost was running over budget in your budget-vs-actual review.
Action
You analysed the spend, benchmarked alternate vendors, renegotiated price / consolidated volume.
Result
"I reduced the cost by ___% / saved ₹___." — put your real figure here.

Story 2 — A tough negotiation

S/T
A key vendor wanted a price increase / was the sole source.
Action
You brought competing quotes, negotiated terms and freight, protected quality.
Result
Held or reduced price; kept supply secure.

Story 3 — Cross-team coordination / problem solved

S/T
Production needed material urgently / stores flagged a shortage.
Action
You coordinated with production, stores and finance, expedited the vendor.
Result
Avoided a line stoppage / delivered on time.

One story, many questions. Your cost-saving story answers: "tell me about an achievement," "a time you saved money," "a time you used data," "your biggest contribution." Your shortage story answers: "a time under pressure," "a conflict," "a problem you solved," "a time you coordinated across teams." Prep 3 stories well and you cover ~15 questions.

Behavioral questions to rehearse with your STAR stories

Tell me about a time you saved costs or improved a process.

→ Use Story 1. Lead with the number. Structure: the budget overrun (S/T), what you analysed and negotiated (A), the % or ₹ saved (R).

Describe a time you worked under pressure or to a tight deadline.

→ Use Story 3. Emphasise staying calm, prioritising by production impact, and clear communication across teams.

Tell me about a conflict or disagreement and how you handled it.

→ A vendor dispute or an internal disagreement (e.g. stores vs production on priority). Show you listened, used facts/data, and reached a workable outcome without damaging the relationship.

Tell me about a mistake you made and what you learned.

Pick something real, low-stakes, and show the lesson. "Early on I once relied on an outdated lead time and it nearly caused a delay. I caught it, expedited, and from then on I made checking and updating master data a habit before every order. It's why I'm careful about data integrity now."

Why this works Ties the lesson straight to the JD's data-integrity requirement. Owning a mistake honestly reads as maturity.
Avoid "I'm a perfectionist" or claiming you've never made a mistake. Both read as evasive.
Tell me about a time you used data to make a decision.

→ Budget-vs-actual analysis or inventory reduction. Show the data you looked at, the insight, the action, the result.

Give an example of working with a cross-functional team.

→ Your coordination with production, stores and finance. The JD values this ("coordinating with production, stores and finance teams") — name those teams explicitly.

HR questions — a one-liner ready for each

These feel easy, which is the trap — vague answers here cost more than they should. Have a crisp, specific line for every one.

What are your strengths?

"Ownership of the full P2P cycle, strong negotiation, data-driven cost analysis, and materials knowledge from my polymer background. I learn fast and I'm reliable under deadline pressure."

Why this works Three job-relevant strengths plus the rare differentiator (materials knowledge). Specific, not generic.
What's your weakness?

"My live work has been on D365 rather than SAP, so I've been actively training in SAP MM to close that gap. I also tend to double-check my data for accuracy, which I'm learning to balance with moving faster."

Why this works Pick a real, fixable weakness and show action on it. The SAP one is smart because it's already known — addressing it head-on is disarming.
Avoid Fake weaknesses ("I work too hard") and anything that's a dealbreaker for the role.
Where do you see yourself in 3–5 years?

"Growing into a senior buyer or category specialist with deep commodity and SAP expertise, owning larger and more strategic sourcing — ideally here, as the business and my responsibilities grow."

Why this works Ambitious but realistic, and rooted in this company so they don't worry you'll leave in a year.
Why should we hire you?

"Strong procurement fundamentals already proven in a real P2P role, a materials background that helps me make better sourcing calls, genuine eagerness to grow into semiconductor procurement, and full comfort with the role's shift and location. I'll ramp fast and take ownership."

Why this works A tight summary of your whole case, and it slips in the shift reassurance again — which they care about.
How do you handle pressure / tight deadlines?

"I prioritise by production impact, communicate early with all stakeholders, and stay calm and methodical. In procurement, deadline pressure is the norm — clear communication and good data keep it manageable."

Are you interviewing elsewhere / do you have other offers?

"I'm exploring a few opportunities in procurement, but this role is the one that genuinely excites me because of the scale and the semiconductor industry. I'm focused on finding the right fit, not just any move."

Why this works Honest, shows you're in demand without bluffing, and reaffirms genuine interest in them.
How soon can you join / what's your notice period?

Answer factually with your actual notice period. If it's long, mention any flexibility (early release, buyout) you'd be open to. "My notice period is ___ days; I'd work with both sides to make the transition as smooth as possible."

Do you have any concerns about the role?

Don't raise the shift or SAP as concerns — you've already shown you're fine with both. Turn it into a question that shows engagement:

"No real concerns — I'm comfortable with the shift and location. I'd just love to understand more about the commodities I'd be handling and how the team is structured, to picture how I'd add value fastest."

11 / THE MONEY TALK

Salary — how to ask for ~10 LPA without flinching

Current CTC: 4.5 LPA. Target: ~10 LPA. That's a ~2.2x jump — ambitious but defensible for a switch into a global semiconductor MNC with a demanding shift. The key is to anchor high, justify with value, and let them counter.

Market reality (Bangalore, 2026)

Buyer / procurement pay in Bangalore varies widely by company tier. Recent benchmarks:

₹3.9–8.2 LTypical procurement analyst range in Bangalore (25th–75th percentile)
~₹10.4 LTop-decile (90th percentile) procurement analyst earners
₹6–7 LAverage for a senior buyer with procurement skills
₹8.5 L+Where global-MNC buyer roles with niche skills tend to sit

Reading: ~10 LPA is at the upper end for the experience level but realistic for a top-tier MNC role with a tough shift and specialised commodity scope. It's a strong ask, not an outlandish one. Big MNCs also add bonus, shift allowance and benefits on top of base.

Your anchor number

₹10–11 LPA

State a slight range starting at or just above your target, so the negotiated landing still lands near 10. If pushed for a single number, say "around 10.5 LPA" and explain why.

What to actually say

"What's your current CTC and expectation?"
The script

"My current CTC is 4.5 LPA. For this role I'm looking in the range of 10 to 11 LPA. I know that's a meaningful step up, and it reflects the bigger scope — a global semiconductor leader, a more demanding commodity portfolio, the 2-to-11 shift and five-day office commitment — plus the SAP and supply-chain growth I'll take on. I'm flexible for the right overall package and open to discussing it."

Why this works It states the number confidently, then immediately justifies it with role-specific value (not personal need), and closes with flexibility so you don't seem rigid.
"That's a big jump from 4.5. Why so high?"
Justify on value, not need

"I understand. My current package reflects a smaller-scale packaging role. This role is a bigger scope at a global MNC, with a late shift and full-office commitment, and procurement here is more complex and business-critical. I'm pricing against the market for this kind of role and the value I'll deliver, not just my current number — and I'm reasonable and open to a fair overall package."

Why this works "Pricing against the market and value, not my current number" is the single most important reframe — it stops them anchoring your offer to your low current CTC.
Avoid Justifying with personal expenses (rent, loans). Always justify with market value and role scope.
"We can offer less than that." / They counter low
Hold ground, stay warm

Don't accept on the spot. Options:

  • Ask about the total package — bonus, shift allowance, benefits — the headline base isn't everything.
  • "I'm very interested in the role. Is there flexibility to get closer to my range? I'd be comfortable at ___." (your real floor)
  • If the number is fixed, ask for a 6-month review or sign-on, and take time to decide — never feel forced to answer immediately.
Why this works Staying warm while holding ground signals you value yourself without being difficult. Asking for the total package often recovers value the base number hides.

Decide your walk-away floor before the interview (e.g. 8.5 LPA) so you negotiate from a clear position, not on the spot.

Three rules for the money talk: never sound desperate or apologetic about the ask · never give a number below what you'd accept · always frame it as the role's value and the market, not your personal expenses. If asked first, it's fine to state your expectation; you don't have to hide it.

12 / YOUR TURN

Smart questions to ask them

Always have 3–4 ready. Asking good questions signals seriousness and that you're evaluating them too. Pick from these.

  • What does success look like for this role in the first six months?
  • Which commodities or material categories would I be buying?
  • Which SAP version and modules does the team use day to day?
  • How is the procurement team structured, and who would I work most closely with?
  • What are the biggest supply-chain challenges the team is solving right now?
  • What growth or learning paths exist for buyers here?
  • How does the team handle onboarding for someone coming from a different ERP?
  • What are the next steps in the process?
Why this matters The "different ERP onboarding" question is clever — it acknowledges your gap while showing you're already thinking about ramping up. The "next steps" question closes strong and keeps momentum.

Avoid leading with leave/holiday questions. Save detailed perks for after they show interest.

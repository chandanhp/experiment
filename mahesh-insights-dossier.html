SECTION 01 · THE MAN

The journey: factory floors to the corner office

From the Silicon Grapevine podcast — his own telling. This is the story that formed the man you're meeting. Know it well enough to connect with it; never recite it at him.

SECTION 02 · THE MAN

His philosophy: the operating system

He has articulated his rules repeatedly, and they're remarkably consistent across a decade of interviews. These aren't platitudes — they're the rubric he will grade you with.

The three rules of sales — his hiring rubric in disguise

Asked by his 12-year-old son what makes a successful salesperson, he answered with three things he says he still lives by:

  1. Make sure they like you. "Be humble, be jolly, be nice." Don't be the person with an attitude.
  2. Learn to earn their trust. Liking isn't trusting — "it's got to come over a period of time."
  3. Don't mess up one and two. "If they're going to do business with you, it's going to be a lot of trust and commitment — and you better deliver to what you promised."

Read that again as an interview rubric: warmth in the first minutes (like), precision plus honesty about what you don't know (trust), and follow-through on anything you commit to — before, during, and after the call (deliver). He will be scoring exactly this, consciously or not.

SECTION 03 · THE MIND

How he thinks about markets & strategy

From the IP-SoC 2026 keynote interview and the 2014 OIP interview — twelve years apart, the same playbook. Understand it and you can talk strategy in his native frame.

The playbook: "thoughtfully first"

"As they say, early bird catches the worm. But you also have to be thoughtfully first — you cannot be first in something people don't need."

His model of a product's life: what's high-end today (2nm) becomes mainstream in three to five years and low-cost in five to seven. So Analog Bits plants its flag at the leading edge — not because that's where the money is now, but because that's where the wave starts.

The economics he actually runs on

  • "You don't make all your money in the first three, four customers — maybe in the seventh and eighth." Leading-edge customers buy credibility and track record; the follow-through customers buy the profits. Patience economics.
  • Proof discipline: multiple test chips, qualified in multiple customer designs, design wins stacked. The second 2nm chip carries ~15 Analog Bits IPs, silicon-proven — and though they're an IP company, they had it manufactured, packaged and tested "as if it's going to be used in a customer environment." An IP vendor productising like a chip company — that's the trust machine.
  • The pattern repeats: "We were doing FinFET ten, twelve years ago — we were first in FinFET. Now the late market is all moving there." In 2014 he was saying 16nm FinFET design was "5 to 8× more complex than 28nm… no guts, no glory" — and their blind-packaged silicon passed every corner. The 2nm story today is the same movie, new node.
  • Focus over size: entered the PLL market last of six or seven vendors and won leadership anyway — "grow fast but not get killed at the market by the big guys; stay our line of focus and course."
  • Foundry pluralism: "We need strong foundries to succeed — so there are choices for customers and for IP suppliers like us." Working TSMC and Intel (18A, RibbonFET era) — deliberately multi-foundry.
He saw chiplets coming in 2014 Talking about IoT a decade ago, he noted die sizes shrinking to "a very tiny chiplet, almost" and IoT customers jumping from old nodes to modern ones needing high-speed connectivity. The man pattern-matches early — which is why his current chiplet-scalability and power-architecture bets deserve to be discussed as pattern, not fashion.

How this maps to India — your bridge

A line that speaks his language"Your own model — leading-edge today, mainstream in three to five years — is exactly the India story. Qualcomm just taped out 2nm designed entirely in India while the fabless cohort designs at 40 to 12 nanometres. The wave you're first on globally is precisely the wave India's design ecosystem is about to ride — and I'd be selling at the point where they meet."

Self-check

Why does Analog Bits invest in being first at 2nm when few customers buy 2nm IP today?
"Thoughtfully first" + lifecycle economics + patience: plant the flag where the wave starts, monetise as it breaks. That's his strategy in one sentence.
SECTION 04 · THE MIND

His technology worldview: power first

From the Intel Foundry Direct Connect interview and the IP-SoC 2026 keynote. This is the argument he makes to the whole industry — and the frame your product answers should live inside.

The core thesis

"Everyone's worried about performance — but in order to optimize for performance, one needs to manage power first, because it's power driving the performance and not the other way around."

That inversion is the whole company in one sentence. Performance is the prize; power is the lever. Every Analog Bits product — PLLs, sensors, droop detectors, LDOs — exists to work that lever.

The problem, as he describes it

  • Chips at the limits: designs are "pushing reticle size limits beyond limits" — and drawing not hundreds but "thousands of amps" through the package bumps. "To many customers, the problems are popping out as something they've never encountered before."
  • Granular regulation: "Every block within the chip has to have regulated power" — down toward SRAM retention voltages — because granular, per-block regulation is "the only way people are going to optimize power."
  • Backside power isn't salvation: it "solves a lot of the signal-integrity and power-distribution problems, but the problem does not go away — it's somewhat mitigated." Even fed from the backside, "everything has to be carefully regulated."
  • The loop: "One has to sense where the power is being consumed, observe it, and give customers the ability to rectify and change course." Sense → observe → correct: the Intelligent Power Architecture in one breath.
  • Co-development: new problems (beyond known glitches and spikes) are emerging as chips grow — "technology has to be developed in conjunction with customers, working with them as partners."

The competitive gap he claims — memorise this one

His own positioning against the giants "The big ecosystem suppliers like Synopsys and Cadence are all focused on CPU architecture, interconnect architecture, standard libraries — but no one talked about power architecture. How are they going to solve the power problems? That's really where Analog Bits will come in." When you sell against the EDA bundles, this is the ground you stand on — in his exact frame.

Self-check

Mahesh asks how you'd position Analog Bits when a customer says "our EDA vendor throws in the analog IP for free." Which answer lives in his frame?
It's his own argument — the power-architecture gap — applied to the objection, without badmouthing anyone. Price-matching and vendor-bashing both fail his rules; the reframe to power wins in his native frame.

SECTION 06 · USE IT

What all this means for your interview

The decisive fact most candidates will miss: before he was a CEO, Mahesh ran sales — all of it, at Virtual Silicon, then two decades of business at Analog Bits. You are being interviewed by a career salesperson. He will grade you the way a sales leader grades a rep.

His rubric, mapped to your behaviour on the call

His ruleWhat he's watching forYour concrete behaviour
1 · Be likedWarmth, humility, energy in the first two minutes; "humble, jolly, nice"Smile early; thank him for the time once; let genuine enthusiasm for the industry show — his own stated love of semiconductors responds to the same in others
2 · Earn trustPrecision, honesty boundaries, no bluffing; how you handle what you don't know"I don't know, here's how I'd find out"; the Interex confidentiality line delivered calmly; one composed self-correction if needed
3 · DeliverWhether your commitments are realCommit to something small and specific in the call ("I'll send you my account-tiering view of the India market by Friday") — then actually do it. Post-interview follow-through is the hidden third round

What he rewards — and what he punishes

Rewards

  • Passion for the industry ("in my blood" energy)
  • Customer-problem framing — every answer anchored in what a buyer needs
  • Resilience told without self-pity — pivots as chapters, not detours
  • Focus: knowing where you won't spend time
  • Shipping: crisp answers now over perfect answers never
  • Ambition stated plainly — dream big is house style

Punishes

  • Bluffing — the fail-fast man forgives error, not pretence
  • Badmouthing Silicon Creations or Interex — trust rules apply to how you treat the other side
  • Spec-perfection paralysis — rambling toward completeness
  • Funding-and-title talk over customer talk
  • Borrowed knowledge worn as your own (wine included)

Three moves this dossier makes possible

  • The attributed reference: one moment of "I watched your Intel Direct Connect conversation — the point about thousands of amps through the bumps reframed how I explain sensors to customers." Diligence, shown once, lands hard.
  • The teaching question: hand him a story he loves telling — "what do you tell young salespeople actually matters?" — and listen like it's the answer to everything. Because in this room, it is.
  • The pattern compliment that's actually analysis: "You were first in FinFET twelve years before the late market arrived; you're running the same play at 2nm and gate-all-around. My read is India's design wave is three years behind your product wave — which makes now exactly the right time to build the channel." That's not flattery; that's strategy in his own playbook's language.
The single sentence to carry into the room He built a bootstrapped company on three rules — be liked, be trusted, deliver — and a single thesis: power first, thoughtfully first. Show him those rules in your behaviour and that thesis in your answers, and you're no longer a candidate; you're a colleague he hasn't hired yet.

Self-check

Given that Mahesh personally ran sales for years, the biggest silent test of the interview is:
The interview IS the sales demo. A career salesperson watches the meta-level: how you open, how you handle the unknown, how you close, whether you follow through. Every answer is content; the call itself is the audition.
